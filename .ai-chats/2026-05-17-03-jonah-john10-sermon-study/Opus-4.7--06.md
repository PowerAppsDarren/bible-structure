# 📖 Lamentations 3:1–18 — "The Man Who Has Seen Affliction"
### A Deep Devotional Study

---

## 🌑 Setting the Stage — Who Is Speaking, and From Where

> *"I am the man that hath seen affliction by the rod of his wrath."* (Lamentations 3:1)

Lamentations was written by **Jeremiah** — the *weeping prophet* — sitting in the smoking ruins of Jerusalem after Babylon sacked the city in **586 BC**. The temple is gone. The walls are rubble. The covenant people have been deported. Children are dying of hunger in the streets (Lam 2:11-12, 4:4). Mothers are doing unspeakable things (Lam 2:20, 4:10).

And Jeremiah — the man who **warned them this would happen for 40 years** and was beaten, jailed, lowered into a cistern, and called a traitor for his trouble — is sitting in the ashes writing **poetry**.

Lamentations is **five acrostic poems**, each chapter using the 22 letters of the Hebrew alphabet — *aleph to tav* — to say: *we are grieving this from A to Z. Every letter has a wound attached to it.*

Chapter 3 is the center of the book — and **the most intense** acrostic. Every Hebrew letter gets *three* verses instead of one. **22 letters × 3 = 66 verses.** When grief is this deep, you don't say it once. You say it three times.

Dr. Bill Creasy points out that the **literary structure itself is the message**: in the middle of formless chaos, Jeremiah imposes the most rigid poetic form in the Bible. *Lament without form becomes despair. Lament with structure becomes worship.*

---

## ⛓️ Verses 1–3 — "Against Me Is His Hand Turned"

> *"I am the man that hath seen affliction by the rod of his wrath. He hath led me, and brought me into darkness, but not into light. Surely against me is he turned; he turneth his hand against me all the day."* (3:1-3)

Jeremiah doesn't blame Babylon. He doesn't blame the false prophets. He doesn't blame the king. **He names God as the source of his suffering.**

The Hebrew word for "rod" — **שֵׁבֶט (shevet)** — is the **same word** used in Psalm 23:4: *"thy rod and thy staff they comfort me."* Same rod. Same God. **In Psalm 23 it comforts. In Lamentations 3 it crushes.** The rod hasn't changed. The man's *experience* of the rod has.

This is one of the most spiritually mature things in the Bible: **Jeremiah refuses to let God off the hook to make himself feel better.** No "the devil made me suffer." No "this is just life." Just: *"Your rod. Your hand. Your wrath."* He addresses the right God.

Job did the same thing. Habakkuk did the same thing. **Real faith doesn't dodge God's sovereignty when it hurts** — it wrestles with it openly.

---

## 🦴 Verses 4–6 — The Bodily Toll

> *"My flesh and my skin hath he made old; he hath broken my bones. He hath builded against me, and compassed me with gall and travail. He hath set me in dark places, as they that be dead of old."* (3:4-6)

Notice how grief becomes **physical**. "Made old." "Broken my bones." "Dark places."

Modern medicine is just now catching up to what Jeremiah knew 2,600 years ago — **prolonged grief and trauma physically age the body.** Cortisol erodes the immune system. Sleep collapses. Bones literally weaken. *"My flesh and my skin hath he made old"* isn't metaphor — it's a clinical observation.

And v. 6 — *"as they that be dead of old"* — Hebrew **כְּמֵתֵי עוֹלָם (k'metei olam)** — *"like the long-dead."* Jeremiah feels like a person who died years ago and just hasn't been buried yet. If you've ever been in a season like that — **you are in good biblical company.** This is not a sign you've lost your faith. It's a sign you're still honest.

---

## 🧱 Verses 7–9 — The Sealed-Off God

> *"He hath hedged me about, that I cannot get out: he hath made my chain heavy. Also when I cry and shout, he shutteth out my prayer. He hath inclosed my ways with hewn stone, he hath made my paths crooked."* (3:7-9)

**This is the part nobody talks about in the worship songs.**

> *"When I cry and shout, he shutteth out my prayer."*

The Hebrew **שָׂתַם (satam)** — to *block up*, *dam off*. Jeremiah says his prayers are hitting a wall. He's shouting and getting **silence back**.

If you've ever prayed and felt like the ceiling was bronze — **Lamentations 3:8 is in your Bible specifically for that moment.** John Bevere has noted that the *fear of the Lord* includes the willingness to keep praying *even when prayer feels useless*, because we trust His character more than we trust our circumstances.

This is also the experience of **Christ on the cross** — *"My God, My God, why hast thou forsaken me?"* (Matt 27:46). Jesus quoted Psalm 22, but He **lived Lamentations 3**. The sealed-off heavens. The prayers that seem to bounce. He's been there. He sympathizes (Heb 4:15).

---

## 🐻 Verses 10–11 — The Predator Imagery

> *"He was unto me as a bear lying in wait, and as a lion in secret places. He hath turned aside my ways, and pulled me in pieces: he hath made me desolate."* (3:10-11)

Jeremiah uses **predator imagery for God** — bear, lion, ambusher. This is one of the most shocking things in Scripture. He says God is *stalking* him.

Two things to hold here:

1. **Jeremiah is describing his perception**, not the final truth about God. He'll correct it in v. 22. But the perception is real, and the Bible records it.
2. **God allowed this poem into the canon.** He didn't edit Jeremiah's grief. The Holy Spirit looked at *"He was unto me as a bear lying in wait"* and said, **"Keep that. Someone in 2026 is going to need to read it."**

You are allowed to feel this way. You are not allowed to *camp* there — but you are allowed to *feel* it on the way through.

---

## 🏹 Verses 12–13 — The Archer God

> *"He hath bent his bow, and set me as a mark for the arrow. He hath caused the arrows of his quiver to enter into my reins."* (3:12-13)

The word "reins" — Hebrew **כִּלְיָה (kilyah)** — literally the **kidneys**, which in ancient Hebrew anthropology was the seat of the **innermost emotions and conscience** (Ps 7:9, Ps 16:7, Jer 17:10). God's arrows haven't grazed Jeremiah's skin — they've gone into the *deepest part of his interior life*.

This is the suffering that isn't physical. It's the wound *under the wound*. The grief in the place no one else can see.

---

## 😢 Verses 14–18 — The Bottom

> *"I was a derision to all my people; and their song all the day. He hath filled me with bitterness, he hath made me drunken with wormwood. He hath also broken my teeth with gravel stones, he hath covered me with ashes. And thou hast removed my soul far off from peace: I forgat prosperity. And I said, My strength and my hope is perished from the LORD..."* (3:14-18)

Let's let v. 18 land:

> **"My strength and my hope is perished from the LORD."**

Hebrew: **אָבַד נִצְחִי וְתוֹחַלְתִּי מֵיְהוָה** — *"my endurance and my expectation have been destroyed by the LORD."*

**This is the bottom of the Bible.** The lowest verse in one of the lowest chapters in Scripture. The prophet of God saying, *"I have no hope left. And the One I lost it to is the LORD."*

If you have ever said this — out loud or in your heart — **the Bible has been there before you.** This isn't weak faith. This is honest faith hitting the floor.

---

## ❄️ The Featured Verse — Why v. 18 Matters So Much

> *"My strength and my hope is perished from the LORD."* (3:18)

Here's why the Holy Spirit included this verse:

Because the famous, most-quoted, hymn-spawning, **"Great Is Thy Faithfulness"** verses are *the very next paragraph* — Lamentations 3:22-23:

> *"It is of the LORD'S mercies that we are not consumed, because his compassions fail not. They are new every morning: great is thy faithfulness."*

**You cannot understand v. 22-23 without v. 18.**

The man who wrote "great is thy faithfulness" wrote it **four verses after writing "my hope is perished."** The hymn we sing at Easter sunrise was born in a man who, *eighty seconds earlier in the poem*, was saying he had nothing left.

Jonathan Cahn often emphasizes the Hebrew pattern of **midnight → morning, sundown → sunrise** — *"And the evening and the morning were the first day"* (Gen 1:5). The biblical day **starts in darkness** and moves toward light. Lamentations 3 is built on that same pattern. **The dawn comes after the dark — but not without the dark.**

So if you are in verses 1–18 right now — if you're in *"the man that hath seen affliction"* — **stay in the poem.** The mercies are coming. The new morning is on the next page. But you have to walk through 18 to get to 22.

The Hebrew word for "perished" in v. 18 — **אָבַד (avad)** — is the same word Jesus uses in the **Parable of the Lost Sheep**: *the sheep which was lost (avad/apollumi)*. The thing that "perishes" in the Bible's vocabulary is also the thing that **gets found**. Hope that has "perished" is hope God specializes in resurrecting.

## 🌍 The 2026 Application

We live in a culture that has no liturgy for grief. We have **influencer reels** and **toxic positivity** and **"good vibes only"** stickers on water bottles. We don't know how to sit in ashes. We don't know how to *not* fix something.

Lamentations 3 gives us permission to:

- **Name the wound** without sanitizing it
- **Address it to God** without losing faith
- **Stay in the poem** without rushing to the resolution
- **Use structure** (Scripture, the Psalms, written prayers, the church calendar) when our feelings have no form

Chuck Missler used to say that *the Bible is the only book where the heroes are allowed to be a mess.* David in the Psalms. Job. Habakkuk. Jeremiah. **Peter on the dock weeping after the rooster crowed.** The Bible doesn't airbrush its prophets. It records their breakdowns *so that ours have company.*

If you walked into church this morning carrying something heavy — *something you couldn't say out loud during worship* — Lamentations 3:1-18 is your liturgy. You can pray these exact words. They are sanctioned by God Himself.

---

## 🐍 The Spiritual Warfare Layer

The enemy's lie in seasons of Lamentations 3 is always one of two things:

1. **"God has abandoned you."** (v. 8 — *"he shutteth out my prayer"*)
2. **"You should never have felt this way. Real Christians don't despair."**

Both are lies.

> *"We are troubled on every side, yet not distressed; we are perplexed, but not in despair; persecuted, but not forsaken; cast down, but not destroyed."* (2 Cor 4:8-9)

Paul felt all of this. He uses the language of pressure and crushing — and immediately follows with **"but not"**. The "but not" is the gospel hinge. *Yes, I am in Lamentations 3:1-18 — but I am not finished. There is a verse 22 coming.*

The enemy wants you to read v. 18 and **close the book.** The Holy Spirit wants you to read v. 18 and **turn the page.**

---

## 🕊️ Closing Reflection

The man speaking in Lamentations 3 is not weak. He's not faithless. He's not in sin. He is the **prophet of God**, the one who told the truth when nobody wanted to hear it, the one who paid the price for being right.

And here he sits — in the ashes of the city he loved, writing the most carefully structured poetry in the Bible, naming his pain *to God*, *to the very God who allowed it.*

**That is what mature faith looks like in the dark.**

It doesn't pretend the dark isn't dark. It doesn't blame someone other than God for the dark. It doesn't deny God in the dark. It **prays in the dark, in form, in honesty, in tears** — and *trusts the morning that hasn't arrived yet.*

> *"It is of the LORD'S mercies that we are not consumed..."* (3:22)

Four verses away.

Stay in the poem. The morning is coming.

What did he remember that turned the poem? That's the question for the next study.
