# Exchange 05: "What problems do you deem too stubborn for God?" — Acts 9

- **Timestamp:** 2026-05-17
- **Context:** Third reflection prompt of the sermon — the user supplied the Scripture anchor (Acts 9 / Saul's conversion).

## User Input

> what problems do you deem too stubborn for God  - acts 9

## AI Response

Acts 9. The chapter that should not exist.

If anyone was *too stubborn for God*, it was **Saul of Tarsus**.

> *"And Saul, yet **breathing out threatenings and slaughter** against the disciples of the Lord..."* (Acts 9:1)

Read that verb again — **ἐμπνέων (empneōn)** — *breathing out.* Murder wasn't something Saul *did*; it was something he **exhaled**. Hating Christians was his respiration. He had letters from the high priest. He had a travel itinerary. He had a kill list with names on it. He was a Harvard-trained Pharisee with the political backing of Jerusalem and a personal vendetta written into his lungs.

**That guy.** That's who Jesus picked.

So when we say *"too stubborn for God,"* we are quietly insulting the resume of the apostle Paul.

---

## The "too stubborn" list we keep

Let's name them, the way the sermon would have us:

🧱 **The family member who's been hostile to the gospel for 30 years.**
*"They'll never change."* Saul's been hostile for less time than that and Jesus knocked him off a horse.

⛓️ **The addiction you've prayed about a thousand times.**
*"It's just how I am."* Saul thought killing Christians was just how he was. **2 Corinthians 5:17** — *"if any man be in Christ, he is a new creature."* The Greek **καινός (kainos)** — not refurbished. *New kind.* Brand-new species.

💔 **The marriage everyone's already given up on.**
*"It's too far gone."* The road to Damascus was *also* too far gone — that's why Saul was on it. The further gone something is, the more spectacular the rescue.

🏚️ **The wayward kid who walked away from church at 17 and is now 47.**
*"They've made their choice."* So had Saul. He'd made it loudly, publicly, professionally. **Three days** of blindness and a man named Ananias later — *"Brother Saul..."* (Acts 9:17). Notice that word. *Brother.* The man who came to arrest you is now your brother. That's how fast God can flip it.

🗳️ **The political enemy you've decided is beyond reach.**
Same Saul. He wasn't just a religious enemy — he was a *state-backed* enforcer with legal authority to imprison believers. The most politically dangerous man in the early church became its greatest missionary. Pray for them. *They* might be Acts 9 waiting to happen.

🕳️ **The version of yourself you think God's done with.**
The sin you keep returning to. The failure that defines your self-talk. The voice that whispers *He gave up on you in 2019.* He didn't. Saul was on his way to *commit more atrocities* when Jesus showed up. God doesn't wait for us to reach a stopping point. He interrupts mid-sentence.

---

## What Acts 9 actually proves

Notice what God says to Ananias when Ananias pushes back (*"Lord, I have heard by many of this man, how much evil he hath done..."* — 9:13):

> *"Go thy way: for he is a **chosen vessel** unto me, to bear my name before the Gentiles, and kings, and the children of Israel."* (Acts 9:15)

While Saul was breathing out murder, **God was already calling him a chosen vessel.** The label came before the change. The plan was in motion before the conversion. **God's "yes" preceded Saul's "yes" by a long, long time.**

That means the people on your "too stubborn" list? **God may already be calling them chosen vessels** while they're still breathing out their version of threats. The label is already over their head. The horse is already on the road. The light is already loaded.

---

## The Ananias question

But here's the part of Acts 9 nobody preaches enough:

**God didn't just need a Saul. He needed an Ananias.**

Some scared, ordinary disciple in Damascus who had to **walk to the address of the man sent to kill him** and lay hands on him and call him *brother.* God could have struck Saul down on the Damascus road alone. Instead He chose to involve a trembling believer with a doorknob in his hand.

So flip the question:

> *Whose Ananias are you refusing to be — because you've decided they're too stubborn for God?*

The prayer you stopped praying.
The conversation you stopped initiating.
The invitation you stopped extending.
The forgiveness you've withheld pending evidence of change.

God's already on the Damascus road of someone in your life. He's looking for an Ananias willing to walk to Straight Street (Acts 9:11) and knock.

---

> *"For with God nothing shall be impossible."* — **Luke 1:37**

The Greek: **οὐκ ἀδυνατήσει παρὰ τοῦ θεοῦ πᾶν ῥῆμα** — *"not any word from God shall be without power."*

Every promise He's made about that "too stubborn" person still carries power. The word hasn't expired. The horse hasn't left. The light still works.

Keep praying. Keep knocking. **Keep being an Ananias.** 🐴⚡
